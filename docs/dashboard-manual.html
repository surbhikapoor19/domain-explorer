Domain Explorer

Point it at a folder of research papers and a spreadsheet of items, and it turns them into an interactive knowledge map — a searchable graph, a similarity landscape, evidence-graded benchmark tables, and an AI copilot that answers questions with sourced, verifiable numbers.

1 Overview

Domain Explorer is domain-agnostic. The screenshots people first see are about robotic grasp planning, but nothing in the tool is grasp-specific — it ships with example domains (grasp planning, motion planning) and you can add your own. Wherever this manual says “items”, read it as the things your corpus is about: methods, algorithms, drugs, materials, models…

1 · Ask & reason

Type a natural-language question. The system retrieves relevant passages, walks a knowledge graph, and an LLM synthesizes a grounded answer with a step-by-step proof of how it got there.

2 · See the landscape (optional view)

A 2-D similarity map (UMAP) groups similar items into clusters. Nearby points = similar items. An optional secondary lens (off by default); the knowledge graph is the primary navigation.

3 · Trust the numbers

Benchmark tables are extracted from the papers' own result tables, each number carrying an evidence grade (A/B/C), a consistency score, and a crop of the source table you can inspect.

4 · Discover the non-obvious

A graph neural network overlays predicted relationships with confidence scores, surfacing connections the papers never stated explicitly.

The one-sentence model

PDFs + a CSV of items  →  extraction & embeddings  →  a knowledge graph, a similarity map, and graded benchmarks  →  an explorable dashboard + a copilot. Swap the PDFs and CSV, rebuild, and the whole experience re-skins to your field.

2 Getting started

No install needed to use a published domain — it's a website. Here's the 60-second orientation.

  1. Open the dashboard. You land on Graph Reasoning (the default view).
  2. Either ask a question in the box at the top, or just explore the knowledge graph and the insight panels below it.
  3. Use the top nav to switch views: Graph Reasoning, Explorer, Benchmarks, and (token-gated) Admin.
  4. Use the Evidence filter in the header (All / Hide weak / Strong only) to hide weak/low-grade numbers across the whole app.
  5. To run the copilot on your own key, open ⚙ Settings and pick a provider (see The AI copilot).
Tip Hover any method/item row in the table or any node in the graph — it spotlights the same item across the table, the graph, and every panel. That cross-highlighting is the fastest way to learn how things connect.

Graph Reasoning — “how are these connected, and what does the literature say?”

The landing view. A live knowledge graph of your corpus (items, papers, techniques, hardware, authors, claims) you can pan, zoom, filter, and click into. Toggle between the documented Knowledge Graph and the model-inferred Predicted Relationships. Below the graph sit cross-highlighting insight panels (techniques, timeline, benchmarks, research-gap matrix).

Type a question and it becomes the AI copilot: it shows the answer, the sub-graph it traversed, the supporting passages, and (for performance questions) the ranked benchmark evidence.

Example Which approaches share a technique with the top-cited item, and how do they differ?

Explorer — “what's the shape of the field?” OPTIONAL · OFF BY DEFAULT

A 2-D UMAP similarity map: each item is a dot, similar items sit close together, and clustering colours the natural families. Adjust attribute weights to re-project the map around what you care about (e.g. weight “input data” higher and watch the clusters re-form). A full, filterable item table sits above it, cross-linked to the map.

Note on UMAP This similarity map is a secondary lens and is off by default (enable it from Admin). The Knowledge Graph is the primary way to navigate. UMAP's 2-D projection is used only here — the graph and the copilot don't depend on it. (The default cluster colours you see across the app come from HDBSCAN in the precompute step; a separate client-side k-means re-clusters live when you change attribute weights. Both are independent of the UMAP layout.)

Benchmarks — “what actually performs best, and can I trust it?”

Leaderboards, head-to-head records, and cross-paper validation — all extracted from the papers' own result tables. Every entry shows:

The header's Evidence filter (All / Hide weak / Strong only) hides anything below your chosen tier so you only see numbers worth trusting.

Admin — “ingest a new corpus / rebuild”

Token-gated. Upload a CSV + PDFs for a new domain, map columns to roles, provide the domain’s knowledge-graph aliases and benchmark metrics, then trigger a cloud build, watch its status, and toggle which views are enabled. This is where a new domain is born (see Add your own domain).

4 Key concepts

A few ideas explained once, used everywhere. Expand any to go deeper.

Evidence grades & confidence (A / B / C)
Extracted numbers are not equal. A grade encodes how much to trust one: A when multiple independent papers report consistent values, B for a single solid source, C when a number is weak or papers disagree. A single 0–1 confidence score (grade + a penalty for high variance) drives the global Evidence filter — pick Hide weak and grade-C / disputed numbers vanish across Benchmarks, the detail panels, and head-to-head.
Cross-highlighting (“everything connects to everything”)
Hovering or selecting an item in any surface — table row, graph node, scatter dot, insight bar — lights up that item everywhere else. Clicking a category (a technique, a year, an author) highlights every item connected to it across all panels. Click empty space, press Esc, or re-click to clear.
Predicted relationships (a graph neural network)
Beyond the relationships literally stated in papers, a heterogeneous graph neural network learns the structure of the corpus and predicts likely-but-undocumented links (e.g. “these two items probably belong in a head-to-head”), each with a confidence score and a min-confidence slider. It's a hypothesis generator, clearly separated from documented facts.
Provenance & proof
Every benchmark number carries its lineage: the source paper, the raw cell text, the table caption, the page, and an image crop of the table it came from. Click Source on any row to verify the number with your own eyes — no black boxes.
Research-gap matrix
A cross-tab of any two attributes from your CSV. Empty cells are coverage gaps — combinations no item in the corpus addresses yet. A fast way to spot open problems / under-explored regions in your field.

5 The AI copilot

The query box on Graph Reasoning. It runs client-side: retrieval + graph traversal + benchmark grounding happen in your browser, and only the final prompt goes to an LLM.

6 Pipeline architecture

How raw PDFs become the dashboard. Every step is precomputed offline into static JSON, so the live site is fast and serverless.

Step by step
1 · Extract. GROBID parses born-digital text into structured TEI (sections, references, in-text citation context). Docling reads the result tables (with bounding boxes, so each number keeps a crop) — OCR off, since papers are born-digital.
2 · RAG. Text is chunked, embedded with a sentence-transformer, and stored in a vector index. (At query time the copilot ranks these chunks with BM25 lexical scoring; the embeddings back semantic search.)
3 · Knowledge graph. Entities (items, papers, techniques, hardware, authors, claims) and their relationships — including citation stance — are assembled into a graph.
4 · HGT. A heterogeneous graph neural network learns the graph and predicts latent links.
5 · Precompute. Writes all the static JSON the pages read (items, knowledge-graph, landing, etc.) to public/data-<domain>/, served statically. It also computes a UMAP 2-D layout + HDBSCAN clusters — but solely to draw the optional Explorer similarity map and colour its dots. Clustering is not used for any inference: not in the knowledge graph, not in the copilot. (When you drag attribute weights, a client-side k-means re-clusters that map live.)
6 · Benchmarks. Table cells are normalized (units, multi-level headers, aliases), de-duplicated per paper, scored for consistency, and assigned A/B/C grades with provenance. This step runs after precompute and is triggered separately (the Admin “Build benchmarks” action / the benchmark step).
How the knowledge graph is built — and how edges work across domains
The graph is assembled from three sources, then normalized:
  • Your CSV → one item node per row, plus an attribute node for each categorical value (a technique, a piece of hardware, a dataset, a year…).
  • GROBID / TEI (paper structure) → paper, author, institution, year, figure, table, equation and reference nodes.
  • LLM extraction over the paper text → contribution, problem, limitation, comparison and method-step nodes.
Edges are typed, and the edge vocabulary is shared across every domain — e.g. described_in, uses_hardware, trained_on, implemented_in, maintained_by, addresses_problem, contributes, compares/outperforms, cites_external, authored_by, co_authored_with, published_in_year, has_figure/table/equation, plus computed semantically_similar / similar_in_role links.

The cross-domain trick: CSV edges are role-driven, not column-name-driven. Each column is mapped to a semantic role (e.g. hardware.platform, train.regime, meta.maintainer, identity.year), and the role — not the literal header — decides the edge type. So a grasp sheet's “End-effector Hardware” column and a motion sheet's “Middleware” column each become the appropriate relationship to their values; a new domain just maps its own columns to roles and the same machinery yields the same edge semantics. Paper-structure edges come from TEI, claim edges from the LLM, and similarity edges from the chunk embeddings.

Finally, per-domain alias tables (Admin → Knowledge-Graph aliases) canonicalize surface forms (rrt*→RRT*, franka→Franka Emika Panda) so one technique / robot / problem isn't fragmented into several nodes.

8 Add your own domain

This is the whole point: the dashboard is a template over a corpus. To bring a new field in, you supply your corpus (a CSV + the PDFs) plus a small one-time vocabulary block, then rebuild.

① A CSV of items

One row per item, columns for the attributes you want to explore and compare (the Explorer table, weights, and research-gap matrix read these directly).

② The PDFs

The papers describing those items. Everything downstream — graph, embeddings, benchmarks — is derived from them.

  1. In Admin, create the domain and upload the CSV + PDFs. An LLM proposes a column→role mapping (title, description, attributes) from your headers; you confirm or adjust it — no code.
  2. In the same editor, optionally provide the small amount of domain vocabulary the intelligence layer needs: Knowledge-Graph aliases (normalize spelling variants of your techniques / hardware / problems to one canonical label) and Benchmark metrics (the metrics, conditions, and datasets to read from result tables). The copilot's ranking vocabulary is then auto-derived from those metric aliases — nothing extra to type. Optionally paste the domain's Google Drive folder of CSV exports to enable the nightly auto-sync (see Keep it live below).
  3. Hit Build. CI runs the main chain (extraction → RAG → graph → HGT → precompute) and commits the precomputed JSON back. Benchmarks are a separate Build benchmarks action (the Docling table extractor, run after precompute), so trigger that too once the main build succeeds.
  4. When both finish, your domain is a first-class, interactive view — the copilot, benchmarks, graph, and map re-skin to your field automatically.
Keep it live (nightly sync) Give a domain a Google Drive folder of CSV exports (set it in Admin when you build the domain). A nightly job pulls the newest CSV from that folder, hash-compares it to the current one, and — only when it changed — commits it and triggers a precompute-only rebuild (no PDF re-ingest; unchanged folders are left untouched). That rebuild recomputes everything CSV-derived: the methods table, the similarity map (UMAP + clusters) and its cluster insight, Research Gaps, Timeline, and Benchmarks. The RAG/copilot corpus and the paper-extracted plots (Most Referenced, Techniques) are preserved unchanged, and the knowledge-graph keeps its existing nodes — a brand-new item gains a graph node only after a full Admin build (which re-ingests its PDF). PDFs are re-ingested only when you trigger that full build.
Safe rebuilds Every precomputed artifact is written through an overwrite guard: a failed or partial rebuild (missing data, a crashed step) can never blank a populated file — the last good data is kept.

What's automatic vs what you provide

Automatic from your CSV + PDFsYou provide once (in Admin)
Branding, column→role rendering, Explorer table / weights / color-by, the similarity map, knowledge-graph structure, RAG retrieval, evidence grades, and the copilot's method summaries. Knowledge-graph alias tables (spelling normalization) and the benchmark metrics + conditions + datasets. The copilot's ranking keywords are derived from these — not typed separately.

How domain-agnostic is it really? (the honest version)

The presentation layer is genuinely agnostic — it reads everything from config, so the chrome re-skins for any field with zero code changes. The intelligence layer (copilot vocabulary, graph entity-normalization, benchmark metrics) is config-driven too, but those configs encode real domain knowledge you supply once in Admin — they are not inferred for free. So the honest claim is “domain-adaptable with a small, one-time vocabulary file,” not “drop in PDFs and everything is perfect.” A second domain — motion planning — is configured end-to-end as the working proof case: its knowledge graph, similarity map, benchmarks, and copilot all run from its own config via exactly the path your field would follow. The richness of the benchmarks still depends on whether your papers report comparable result tables — that is a property of the corpus, not the engine.
